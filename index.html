01 - Thesis

AI is a task shock before it is a jobs shock.

By 2027-2030, artificial intelligence is unlikely to create a simple economy-wide labour collapse. The more likely near-term outcome is a productivity divide. Firms, workers and countries that can integrate AI into workflows will gain a compounding advantage, while routine cognitive workers, junior knowledge roles and smaller firms face task erosion, weaker bargaining power and exclusion from productivity gains unless policy improves diffusion, training and competition.

Forecast in one sentence: AI will increase output potential, but the distribution of gains will depend on adoption capacity, complementary investment, labour-market adjustment, market structure and governance.

Why 2027-2030?

This horizon is close enough for current generative AI adoption, workplace redesign and data-centre investment to matter, but long enough for firms, workers and governments to respond.

How AI 2027 is used

AI 2027 is used as a scenario inspiration source, not as a certain prediction. It is useful because it demonstrates how AI futures can branch depending on capability progress, governance and adoption speed.

02 - Evidence base

Current evidence points to exposure, not instant replacement.

Labour exposure

The IMF estimates that almost 40% of global employment is exposed to AI, rising to about 60% in advanced economies. Exposure means jobs are affected, not automatically replaced.

Australian labour market

Jobs and Skills Australia argues that generative AI is more likely to augment many jobs than simply replace them, although impacts vary by occupation, industry and region.

Firm adoption

Many firms are experimenting with AI, but the economic impact depends on whether they redesign workflows around the technology rather than simply adding AI tools to existing processes.

Infrastructure limits

AI is not purely digital. It depends on data centres, electricity, chips and cloud infrastructure, which can become constraints on productivity growth.

03 - Forecasting logic

From AI capability to economic outcomes

AI capability does not automatically become productivity growth. Economic impact occurs only when capability passes through adoption, task redesign, labour adjustment and governance.

1

Capability

AI improves at writing, coding, search, analysis, customer service and decision support.

2

Task cost falls

The time and cost of routine cognitive tasks declines.

3

Work redesign

Firms automate some tasks and augment others with AI copilots and agents.

4

Productivity

Output rises where firms have data, skills, trust, compute and management capacity.

5

Distribution

Gains flow to AI-complementary workers, capital owners and dominant platforms unless policy broadens access.

Simple forecasting identity: Economic impact = AI capability × adoption × task exposure × complementary investment × governance quality.

04 - Scenarios

Three plausible economic futures

Baseline

Uneven augmentation

AI becomes common, but adoption is uneven. Large firms and high-skill workers benefit first because they have better data, software budgets and management systems.

  • Productivity: moderate gains concentrated in knowledge-intensive firms.
  • Tasks: routine writing, research and customer support are compressed.
  • Wages: AI-complementary workers gain more bargaining power.
  • Structure: large firms strengthen their advantage.

Upside

Broad productivity diffusion

AI becomes cheap, safe and useful for SMEs, education, healthcare, public services and regional firms. Policy helps workers move into AI-complementary roles.

  • Productivity: stronger and more widely shared growth.
  • Tasks: AI frees workers for judgement, care and relationship work.
  • Wages: retraining spreads benefits more evenly.
  • Structure: smaller firms gain access to advanced capabilities.

Downside

Concentrated automation race

AI capability improves faster than institutions adapt. Firms automate aggressively, labour adjustment is slow, and compute and data concentrate around dominant platforms.

  • Productivity: high in frontier firms but weakly diffused.
  • Tasks: junior and routine cognitive roles shrink quickly.
  • Wages: exposed workers lose bargaining power.
  • Structure: market concentration increases.

06 - Policy and governance

Policy changes the probability of each scenario.

AI policy should not simply slow or accelerate adoption. The stronger objective is to broaden productivity diffusion while managing displacement, concentration and infrastructure pressure.

07 - Uncertainty and limitations

What could break this forecast?

Capability uncertainty

AI agents may become reliable enough to automate full workflows, or they may remain useful mainly as copilots that still require human judgement.

Adoption uncertainty

Widespread AI use does not guarantee productivity growth. The key question is whether firms redesign work around AI or simply add tools to existing processes.

Measurement uncertainty

Productivity gains may arrive with a lag because training, complementary investment and organisational change take time.

Distribution uncertainty

AI can raise total output while worsening inequality if gains flow mainly to capital owners, high-skill workers and dominant platforms.

Final forecast: The baseline is not mass unemployment by 2030. It is growth with a distribution problem. AI raises productivity where adoption is effective, but without strong diffusion policy the gains are likely to concentrate among larger firms, AI-complementary workers and capital owners.
